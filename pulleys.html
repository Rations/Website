Ropes and Pulleys

Atwood's machine

Suppose we have an Atwood's machine: a massless pulley over which two different masses, \(m\) and \(M\), hang via a massless string. How might we find the accelerations of the masses?

First, let us take the meticulous path to the solution. (We will learn a quicker way, but the meticulous way is useful because it can be applied to more-complicated scenarios.)

Defining upwards to be positive, we can write force equations1:

\begin{align} T - mg &= ma_m \\ T - Mg &= Ma_M \\ \end{align}

Conservation of string

Never overlook physical constraints introduced by the objects in our problem. Consider, for instance, our rope. It is always taut, meaning it will not compress or stretch much. This implies that two objects connected by the rope will accelerate at the same magnitude!

We can show this in a math-y way using calculus. Divide the rope's length \(l\) into three parts as shown in the diagram:

We know the length of the rope must remain constant over time. Thus, differentiating reveals

\begin{align} l &= x + \pi R + y \\ 0 &= \frac{dx}{dt} + 0 + \frac{dy}{dt} \\ 0 &= \frac{d^2x}{dt^2} + \frac{d^2y}{dt^2} \\ a_m &= -a_M \\ \end{align}

So we have used a physical constraint to find a relationship between the accelerations2. Now the system of equations becomes easy to solve.

\begin{align} T - mg &= ma_m \\ T - Mg &= M\left(-a_m\right) \\ ma_m + mg &= -Ma_m + Mg \\ a_m\left(m + M\right) &= g\left(M - m\right) \\ \end{align}
$$a_m = g\left(\frac{M - m}{M + m}\right)$$
$$a_M = -g\left(\frac{M - m}{M + m}\right)$$
\(m\) = kg
\(M\) = kg
\(a_m\) = m/s/s
\(a_M\) = m/s/s

The quicker way

Recognizing the shortcut takes place at the conservation of string step. Since we know that the string, \(m\), and \(M\) all move and accelerate at the same rates, we can group them into one system traveling in the clockwise direction. The total mass is \(m + M\), and \(T\) becomes irrelevant because we already know the relationship between the \(a_m\) and \(a_M\). Thus, by Newton's second law after defining clockwise to be positive:

\begin{align} Mg - mg &= \left(M + m\right)a \\ a &= g\left(\frac{M - m}{M + m}\right) \\ \end{align}

No surprise: both masses accelerate "clockwise" around the pulley.

Double Atwood's machine

What if we keep a mass \(m_1\) hanging from the left side of the pulley, but replace the right mass with a whole new Atwood's machine on which hang \(m_2\) and \(m_3\)?

We can't take the same shortcut we did in the problem of the single Atwood's machine, because different objects in the system move at different rates. Here, we have several force equations:

\begin{align} T - m_1g &= m_1a_1 \\ T - 2T_2 &= (0)a_\text{pulley} = 0 \\ T_2 - m_2g &= m_2a_2 \\ T_2 - m_3g &= m_3a_3 \\ \end{align}

Notice that the second pulley must be taken into consideration, since it is accelerating too. Because it is massless, the sum of the forces on it must be zero (otherwise it would accelerate at an infinite rate).

Now for the tricky part. Like last time, we need to find a relationship between the accelerations, in this case \(a_1\), \(a_2\), and \(a_3\). Since the upper pulley is effectively fixed to the ceiling, we can use conservation of string to find the relation between the \(a_1\) and \(a_\text{pulley}\):

$$a_1 = -a_\text{pulley}$$

It is extremely tempting to do the same for \(m_2\) and \(m_3\) on the lower pulley and say \(a_2 = -a_3\). But this is only true in relation to the bottom pulley, which is itself accelerating because of \(m_1\).

One way to find the relationship between the pulleys is using intuition and adding accelerations. Let's say the lower pulley has acceleration \(a_p = -a_1\), remembering that we can apply conservation of string on the top string because the top pulley is fixed to the ceiling. Let's then say that \(m_2\) and \(m_3\) have accelerations \(a_{2\text{, p}}\) and \(a_{3\text{, p}} = -a_{2\text{, p}}\) relative to the pulley, which has \(a_p\) itself. Thus, their accelerations relative to the Earth are:

\begin{align} a_2 &= a_{2\text{, p}} + a_p \\ a_3 &= -a_{2\text{, p}} + a_p \\ \end{align}

Eliminating \(a_{2\text{, p}}\) and simplifying, we have:

\begin{align} a_2 - a_p &= a_p - a_3 \\ a_p &= \frac{a_2 + a_3}{2} \\ a_1 &= -\frac{a_2 + a_3}{2} \\ \end{align}

The most methodical way, however, involves drawing a diagram and examining the relationships between distances:

From geometry, we can see that the length \(c\) can be written in terms of the others.

\begin{align} c &= \frac{b + d + l - \pi R}{2} \\ \end{align}

Differentiating twice essentially returns a constraint equation comprising the accelerations we are interested in.

\begin{align} \frac{d^2c}{dt^2} &= \frac{1}{2}\left(\frac{d^2b}{dt^2} + \frac{d^2d}{dt^2} + 0 - 0\right) \\ a_\text{pulley} &= -a_1 = \frac{a_2 + a_3}{2} \\ a_1 &= -\frac{a_2 + a_3}{2} \\ \end{align}

Now that we have all the necessary equations, it is simply a matter of algebra before we find the final answers.

\begin{align} T &= m_1a_1 + m_1g \\ T_2 &= \frac{T}{2} = \frac{m_1a_1}{2} + \frac{m_1g}{2} \\ -2a_1 &= a_2 + a_3 \\ a_2 &= \frac{T_2 - m_2g}{m_2} \\ &= \frac{m_1a_1}{2m_2} + \frac{m_1g}{2m_2} - g \\ \end{align}

In the same way,

\begin{align} a_3 &= \frac{m_1a_1}{2m_3} + \frac{m_1g}{2m_3} - g \\ \end{align}

Thus:

\begin{align} -2a_1 &= \frac{m_1a_1}{2m_2} + \frac{m_1g}{2m_2} - g + \frac{m_1a_1}{2m_3} + \frac{m_1g}{2m_3} - g \\ -2a_1 - \frac{m_1a_1}{2m_2} - \frac{m_1a_1}{2m_3} &= \frac{m_1g}{2m_2} + \frac{m_1g}{2m_3} - 2g \\ a_1 \left(-2 - \frac{m_1}{2m_2} - \frac{m_1}{2m_3}\right) &= g\left(\frac{m_1}{2m_2} + \frac{m_1}{2m_3} - 2\right) \\ -a_1\left(\frac{m_1(m_2 + m_3) + 4m_2m_3}{2m_2m_3}\right) &= g\left(\frac{m_1(m_2 + m_3) - 4m_2m_3}{2m_2m_3}\right) \\ \end{align}
$$a_1 = g\frac{4m_2m_3 - m_1(m_2 + m_3)}{4m_2m_3 + m_1(m_2 + m_3)}$$

Phew!

\(m_1\) = kg
\(m_2\) = kg
\(m_3\) = kg
\(a_1\) = m/s/s
\(a_2\) = m/s/s
\(a_3\) = m/s/s

Notes

1 The tension \(T\) must be the same at all points along the rope because the rope is massless. Given a small piece of rope, if the tensions on either side of this rope were unequal, then the piece would accelerate at an infinite rate.

2 For example, \(\frac{d^2 b}{dt^2}\) is the acceleration of the portion of the rope connected to \(m_2\). Since the rope is always rigid, \(\frac{d^2 b}{dt^2} = a_2\).
