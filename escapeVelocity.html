Escape Velocity

Even if you jump as hard as you can, you immediately land back on Earth thanks to gravity. But given enough speed, it is clearly possible for a spaceship, missile, or quasi–manhole cover to escape the gravitational pull of the Earth. How can we find this speed?

Gravitational potential energy

The simplest way is to use energy conservation. To do this, we need a general expression for gravitational potential energy.

Remember that potential energy is a relative measurement: you can set \(PE = 0\) at any origin \(O\) that's useful to you—as long as you calculate your other potential energy measurements with respect to \(O\). When we are working with small objects in the reference frame of the Earth, it is useful to set \(PE = 0\) at the Earth's surface. But when dealing with things of astronomical proportions, it is often convenient to set \(PE = 0\) at a distance \(\infty\) away from the source of gravity (e.g., a planet).

This makes sense: when two objects are infinitely apart, there is no attraction between them (as \(r \to \infty\), \(F_g \to 0\)).

Suppose we have a particle of mass \(m\) sitting on a planet of mass \(M\). We know that if a mass is at some point \(R\), its gravitational potential energy \(U_g\) is equal to the work you would have to do to move it from \(O\) (i.e., \(\infty\)) to \(R\). Using Newton's law of universal gravitation, we can find the force between \(m\) and \(M\), and thus \(U_g\).

\begin{align} U_g &= \int_{r_0}^{r} F_gdr \\ &= \int_{\infty}^{R} G\frac{Mm}{r^2}dr \\ &= GMm \left.\left(-\frac{1}{r}\right|_{\infty}^{R}\right) \\ &= -\frac{GMm}{R} \\ \end{align}

Escape velocity

We assume energy is conserved throughout the object's flight. At launch, the object has both kinetic and potential energy. It needs to have enough total energy to reach a point where its potential energy is zero in order not to "fall back" towards the planet:

$$KE_0 + PE_0 = KE$$

It doesn't matter what velocity we have at the end. We should be alright as long as there is no potential energy. We are trying to find the cutoff, the minimum velocity that will allow the object to escape, so we can set \(v\) and thus \(KE\) to zero.

\begin{align} KE_0 + PE_0 &= 0 \\ \frac{1}{2}m{v_e}^2 - \frac{GMm}{R} &= \\ \end{align}
$$v_e = \sqrt{\frac{2GM}{R}}$$
\(M\) = planet mass = kg
\(R\) = planet radius = m
\(v_e\) = m/s

Note that \(v_e\) is independent of the direction of launch or the mass of the object.

The case of two planets

What if we are transported to a binary planet system, where two planets orbit about each other? Would the escape velocity of a mass on the surface of one of the planets be different?

Fortunately, the problem is not too difficult to tackle. It turns out that we can analyze the gravitational potential energy the mass in relation to each of the planets separately. If the object rests on planet 1,

$$U_{1} = -\frac{GM_1m}{R}$$

. . . just like in the previous problem.

Now we must find \(U_2\). All we need to know is the distance \(D\) between the mass and the center of planet 2:

$$U_{2} = -\frac{GM_2m}{D}$$

Now, setting total energy to be zero again,

\begin{align} KE_0 + U_1 + U_2 &= 0 \\ \frac{1}{2}m{v_e}^2 - \frac{GM_1m}{R} -\frac{GM_2m}{D} &= \\ \end{align}

$$v_e = \sqrt{2G\left(\frac{M_1}{R} + \frac{M_2}{D}\right)}$$
\(M_1\) = planet 1 mass = kg
\(M_2\) = planet 2 mass = kg
\(R\) = planet 1 radius = m
\(D\) = dist. between object and planet 2 = m
\(v_e\) = m/s
