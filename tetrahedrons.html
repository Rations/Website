Tetrahedron Angles

A pyramid with all equilateral-triangle faces, the regular tetrahedron is, in my opinion, one of the most aesthetically appealing solids. In fact, it appears quite frequently in nature.

Finding the face-vertex-edge angle

Divide the base triangle into three smaller triangles formed by intersecting the three angle bisectors at the center. By 30-60-90 triangle properties, we have:

$$b = \left(\frac{0.5a}{\sqrt{3}}\right)2$$ $$b = \frac{a\sqrt{3}}{3}$$ $$\cos\alpha = \frac ba$$ $$\alpha = \arccos \frac{a\sqrt{3}}{3a} = \arccos \frac{\sqrt{3}}{3} = 54.7°$$
$$\alpha = 54.7°$$

Finding the face-edge-face angle

Draw a plane through one of the vertices. From 30-60-90 triangles again, we have:

$$h = \left(\frac a2\right)\sqrt{3} = \frac{a\sqrt{3}}{2}$$

Now we have a triangle with one side length \(a\) and two sides length \(h\). The most intuitive proceeding to me involves geometry. Consider the sub-triangle with base \(0.5a\) and hypotenuse \(h\).

$$\sin\beta = \frac{0.5a}{h}$$ $$\beta = \arcsin \frac{1}{\sqrt{3}}$$
$$\theta = 70.5°$$

Finding the tetrahedral angle

Credit for this extremely elegant approach goes to W. E. Brittin of the University of Colorado, Boulder.

Connect the tetrahedron's centroid to its vertices using four vectors with length \(v\)—\(\mathbf{v_1}\), \(\mathbf{v_2}\), etc. (Alternatively, imagine grabbing four identical angry arrows by the tail and letting them repel as far away from each other as they can.) Thus:

  • \(v = |\mathbf{v_1}| = |\mathbf{v_2}| = |\mathbf{v_3}| = |\mathbf{v_4}|\)
  • \(\mathbf{v_1} + \mathbf{v_2} + \mathbf{v_3} + \mathbf{v_4} = 0\)
  • \(\theta\) between any two vectors is the same

Now, recall the dot product operation: \(\mathbf{v_x} \cdot \mathbf{v_y} = |\mathbf{v_x}||\mathbf{v_y}|\cos\beta\), where \(\beta\) is the angle between the vectors. What happens when you dot any two of our four vectors? Depending on which two you choose, two cases are possible:

  1. \(\mathbf{v_x} \cdot \mathbf{v_y} = v^2\) if you dot a vector \(\mathbf{v}\) with itself, since \(|\mathbf{v}||\mathbf{v}|\cos 0 = v^2\)
  2. \(\mathbf{v_x} \cdot \mathbf{v_y} = v^2\cos\theta\) otherwise

Now, let us slyly manipulate the following equation by dotting both sides with \(\mathbf{v_1}\) (or any of the four of your choosing):

$$\mathbf{v_1} + \mathbf{v_2} + \mathbf{v_3} + \mathbf{v_4} = 0$$ $$\mathbf{v_1} \cdot (\mathbf{v_1} + \mathbf{v_2} + \mathbf{v_3} + \mathbf{v_4}) = \mathbf{v_1} \cdot 0$$ $$\mathbf{v_1} \cdot \mathbf{v_1} + \mathbf{v_1} \cdot \mathbf{v_2} + \mathbf{v_1} \cdot \mathbf{v_3} + \mathbf{v_1} \cdot \mathbf{v_4} = 0$$

The first term in our manipulated equation evaluates to \(v^2\), as per case 1. The other three terms fall under case 2. Therefore:

$$v^2 + 3v^2\cos\theta = 0$$ $$\theta = \arccos \left(-\frac{1}{3}\right)$$
$$\theta = 109.5°$$
