Tetrahedron Angles

A pyramid with all equilateral-triangle faces, the regular tetrahedron is, in my opinion, one of the most aesthetically appealing solids. In fact, it appears quite frequently in nature.

The face-vertex-edge angle, \(\angle DBC\)

By 30-60-90-triangle properties, we have:

\begin{align} BE &= 2 \left(\frac{0.5s}{\sqrt{3}}\right) = \frac{s\sqrt{3}}{3} \\ \alpha &= \arccos\left(\frac{BE}{s}\right) = \arccos \left(\frac{\sqrt{3}}{3}\right) \\ \end{align}
$$\alpha \approx 54.7°$$

The face-edge-face angle

Draw a plane through one of the vertices. From 30-60-90-triangle properties again, we have:

$$h = \frac{a\sqrt{3}}{2}$$

Now we have formed a triangle with side lengths \(a\), \(h\), and \(h\). Using geometry to find the angle in question is then easy. Here is one way: consider the sub-triangle with base \(0.5a\) and hypotenuse \(h\).

$$\beta = \arcsin \left(\frac{0.5a}{h}\right) = \arcsin \left(\frac{1}{\sqrt{3}}\right)$$
$$\theta \approx 70.5°$$

The tetrahedral angle

Credit for this extremely elegant approach goes to W. E. Brittin of the University of Colorado, Boulder.

Connect the tetrahedron's centroid to its vertices using four vectors with length \(v\): \(\mathbf{v}_1\), \(\mathbf{v}_2\), \(\mathbf{v}_3\), \(\mathbf{v}_4\). (Alternatively, imagine grabbing four identical angry arrows by the tail and having them repel from each other as far as they can.) Thus:

  • \(v = |\mathbf{v}_1| = |\mathbf{v}_2| = |\mathbf{v}_3| = |\mathbf{v}_4|\)
  • \(0 = \mathbf{v}_1 + \mathbf{v}_2 + \mathbf{v}_3 + \mathbf{v}_4 \)
  • \(\theta\) between any two vectors is the same

Now, recall the dot product operation: \(\mathbf{v}_x \cdot \mathbf{v}_y = |\mathbf{v}_x||\mathbf{v}_y|\cos\beta\), where \(\beta\) is the angle between the vectors. What happens when you dot any two of our four vectors? Depending on which two you choose, two cases are possible:

  1. \(\mathbf{v}_x \cdot \mathbf{v}_y = v^2\) if you dot a vector \(\mathbf{v}\) with itself, since \(|\mathbf{v}||\mathbf{v}|\cos 0 = v^2\)
  2. \(\mathbf{v}_x \cdot \mathbf{v}_y = v^2\cos\theta\) otherwise

Now, let us slyly manipulate the following equation by dotting both sides with \(\mathbf{v}_1\) (or any of the four of your choosing):

\begin{align} 0 &= \mathbf{v}_1 + \mathbf{v}_2 + \mathbf{v}_3 + \mathbf{v}_4 \\ \mathbf{v}_1 \cdot 0 &= \mathbf{v}_1 \cdot \left(\mathbf{v}_1 + \mathbf{v}_2 + \mathbf{v}_3 + \mathbf{v}_4 \right) \\ 0 &= \mathbf{v}_1 \cdot \mathbf{v}_1 + \mathbf{v}_1 \cdot \mathbf{v}_2 + \mathbf{v}_1 \cdot \mathbf{v}_3 + \mathbf{v}_1 \cdot \mathbf{v}_4 \\ \end{align}

The first term in our manipulated equation evaluates to \(v^2\), as per case 1. The other three terms fall under case 2. Therefore:

\begin{align} 0 &= v^2 + 3v^2\cos\theta \\ \theta &= \arccos \left(-\frac{1}{3}\right) \\ \end{align}
$$\theta \approx 109.5°$$
