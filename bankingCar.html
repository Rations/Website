Banking Car

While driving your Prius, you encounter a turn. A law-abiding citizen, you obey the speed limit and rest assured that you will complete the turn without issue. But what if you were an extreme driver? Would you really be risking your safety by rounding the turn at an improper speed?

1. Slow down, you move too fast!

Some background info: Many turns in roads are banked, or sloped. Jumping into the physics will help us see why.

There are three main forces acting on our car as it rounds the turn: the gravitational force \(F_g\), the normal force \(N\), and the static friction force \(f\).

To stay on the banked turn, this combination of forces must satisfy two conditions:

  1. There must be a net radial force towards the center of the curve such that \(F_{\text{rad}} = \frac{mv^2}{r}\).
  2. You don't want to slide down or up the bank, so the net force in the vertical axis must be zero.

Because we are looking for the boundary ("extreme") cases, we can utilize \(f_\text{max} = \mu N\). Then, examining our free-body diagram, we can translate words into equations:

$$F_\text{rad} = \frac{mv^2}{r} = N\sin\theta + \mu N\cos\theta$$ $$F_g = mg = N\cos\theta - \mu N\sin\theta$$

We want to eliminate \(N\), so using substitution to solve the system is in order. Starting from the second equation and then substituting into the first:

$$N = \frac{mg}{\cos\theta - \mu\sin\theta}$$ $$\frac{mv^2}{r} = \frac{mg(\sin\theta + \mu\cos\theta)}{\cos\theta - \mu\sin\theta}$$
$$v_\text{max} = \sqrt{\frac{rg(\sin\theta + \mu\cos\theta)}{\cos\theta - \mu\sin\theta}}$$
\(r\) = radius = m
\(\theta\) = bank angle = °
\(\mu_\text{sf}\) = coefficient of static friction =
\(v_\text{max}\) = m/s

2. Speed up, you . . . move too slow?

Naturally, you might wonder, Is there a minimum speed?

Probably the easiest way to justify the fact that a minimum speed limit exists is thinking about a car parked on a steep slope—the most extreme case of "slow-moving car" there is. You can easily imagine this parked car slipping. Indeed, the only thing preventing it from doing so is static friction.

Drawing a free-body diagram may help. The most important thing to notice is that, in the case of the slow-moving car, static friction helps the car "fight" gravity by acting upwards along the bank. Once this is clear, the computations should be very familiar:

$$F_\text{rad} = \frac{mv^2}{r} = N\sin\theta - \mu N\cos\theta$$ $$F_g = mg = N\cos\theta + \mu N\sin\theta$$ $$N = \frac{mg}{\cos\theta + \mu\sin\theta}$$ $$\frac{mv^2}{r} = \frac{mg(\sin\theta - \mu\cos\theta)}{\cos\theta + \mu\sin\theta}$$
$$v_\text{min} = \sqrt{\frac{rg(\sin\theta - \mu\cos\theta)}{\cos\theta + \mu\sin\theta}}$$
\(r\) = radius = m
\(\theta\) = angle = °
\(\mu_\text{sf}\) = coefficient of static friction =
\(v_\text{min}\) = m/s

♛

3. The Invincible \(\mu\)

Wait. You've seen cars parked on slopes without slipping. Clearly, it seems possible for a friction force to counteract gravity entirely. Considering a free-body diagram of a stationary car on a bank with angle \(\theta\), we can easily solve for the minimum friction force and therefore friction coefficient required.

In the plane parallel to the slope:

$$f = \mu N = mg\sin\theta$$

In the plane perpendicular to the slope:

$$N = mg\cos\theta$$

Therefore:

$$\mu mg\cos\theta = mg\sin\theta$$
$$\mu = \tan\theta$$

. . . meaning if \(\mu \geq \tan\theta\), we need not worry about a minimum speed at all!

♛

4. What about flat roads?

Due to costs, lagging infrastructure, practicality, etc., not all roads are banked. How do turns in flat roads compare to their banked counterparts? Again, a free-body diagram makes determining our force equations easy:

$$N = mg$$ $$F_\text{rad} = \frac{mv^2}{r} = \mu N = \mu mg$$
$$v_\text{max} = \sqrt{\mu rg}$$

Dividing the \(v_\text{max}\) expression from part 1 by this one suggests that a turn with bank \(\theta\) would permit a maximum velocity \(\sqrt{\frac{\sin\theta + \mu\cos\theta}{\mu (\cos\theta - \mu\sin\theta)}}\) times greater than a flat road would.
