Banking Car

While driving your Prius, you encounter a turn. A law-abiding citizen, you obey the speed limit and rest assured that you will complete the turn without issue. But what if you were an extreme driver? Would you really be risking your safety by rounding the turn at an improper speed?

Slow down, you move too fast!

Some background info: Many turns in roads are banked, or sloped. Jumping into the physics will help us see why.

There are three main forces acting on our car as it rounds the turn: the gravitational force \(F_g\), the normal force \(N\), and the static friction force \(f\).

To stay on the banked turn, this combination of forces must satisfy two conditions:

  1. There must be a net radial force towards the center of the curve such that \(F_{\text{rad}} = \frac{mv^2}{r}\).
  2. You don't want to slide down or up the bank, so the net force in the vertical axis must be zero.

Because we are looking for the boundary ("extreme") cases, we can utilize \(f_\text{max} = \mu N\). Then, examining our FBD, we can translate words into equations:

\begin{align} \frac{mv^2}{r} &= N\sin\theta + \mu N\cos\theta \\ mg &= N\cos\theta - \mu N\sin\theta \\ \end{align}

We want to eliminate \(N\), so using substitution to solve the system of equations is in order. Starting from the second equation and then substituting into the first:

\begin{align} N &= \frac{mg}{\cos\theta - \mu\sin\theta} \\ \frac{mv^2}{r} &= \frac{mg\left(\sin\theta + \mu\cos\theta\right)}{\cos\theta - \mu\sin\theta} \\ \end{align}
$$v_\text{max} = \sqrt{\frac{rg\left(\sin\theta + \mu\cos\theta\right)}{\cos\theta - \mu\sin\theta}}$$

Speed up, you . . . move too slow?

Naturally you might wonder, is there a minimum speed?

Probably the easiest way to justify the fact that a minimum speed limit could exist is thinking about a stationary car on a steep slope—the most extreme case of "slow-moving car" there is. You could easily imagine this parked car slipping. Indeed, the only thing that can prevent it from doing so is static friction.

Again, drawing a FBD helps:

The most important thing to notice is that in the case of the slow-moving car, static friction now helps the car "fight" gravity by acting upwards along the bank. Once you understand this, the computations are familiar:

\begin{align} \frac{mv^2}{r} &= N\sin\theta - \mu N\cos\theta \\ mg &= N\cos\theta + \mu N\sin\theta \\ N &= \frac{mg}{\cos\theta + \mu\sin\theta} \\ \frac{mv^2}{r} &= \frac{mg(\sin\theta - \mu\cos\theta)}{\cos\theta + \mu\sin\theta} \\ \end{align}
$$v_\text{min} = \sqrt{\frac{rg(\sin\theta - \mu\cos\theta)}{\cos\theta + \mu\sin\theta}}$$

The Invincible \(\mu\)

Wait—you've seen cars parked successfully on slopes. Clearly, it seems possible for a friction force to counteract gravity entirely. Examining a FBD, we can easily solve for the smallest maximum static friction force \(f_\text{max} = \mu N\) and static friction coefficient \(\mu\) we need given a bank of angle \(\theta\).

If the car is in equilibrium,

\begin{align} N &= mg\cos\theta \\ \mu N &= mg\sin\theta \\ \end{align}
$$\mu = \tan\theta$$

. . . meaning if \(\mu \geq \tan\theta\), we need not worry about a minimum speed at all!

What about flat roads?

Due to costs, lagging infrastructure, practicality issues, etc., not all roads are banked. How do turns on flat roads compare to their banked counterparts? Again, a FBD makes determining our force equations easy:

Knowing that our car is in uniform circular motion on one plane,

\begin{align} N &= mg \\ \frac{m{v_\text{max}}^2}{r} &= \mu N \\ \end{align}
$$v_\text{max} = \sqrt{\mu rg}$$

Dividing the \(v_\text{max}\) expression from part 1 by this one suggests that a turn with bank \(\theta\) would permit a maximum velocity \(\sqrt{\frac{\sin\theta + \mu\cos\theta}{\mu (\cos\theta - \mu\sin\theta)}}\) times greater than a flat road would.
